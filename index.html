Exacerbation & Inpatient Meds

During hospitalization
Make sure acute treatment is appropriate and chronic therapy is not forgotten.
  • Scheduled SABA ¬± SAMA while unstable; avoid duplicate long-acting bronchodilators.
  • Continue or restart maintenance LABA/LAMA when clinically appropriate.

  • Counsel: morning dosing, with food; warn about insomnia, mood, hyperglycemia.

3. How to Use Your Inhaler

For most inhalers:

  • Stand or sit up straight.
  • Breathe out slowly, away from the inhaler.
  • Put the mouthpiece in your mouth and seal your lips around it.
  • Breathe in as your provider showed you, then hold your breath for about 10 seconds.
  • Breathe out slowly.

Ask your nurse, doctor, or pharmacist to watch you use your inhaler before you leave the hospital. It is okay to ask questions.

4. COPD Zones ‚Äì When to Act

Green Zone ‚Äì Doing well

  • Breathing is your usual ‚Äúnormal.‚Äù
  • Usual cough and mucus.
  • You can do daily activities.

Keep using your daily inhalers as prescribed.

Yellow Zone ‚Äì Warning sign (Call your clinic)

  • More shortness of breath than usual.
  • More cough, or mucus looks thicker or changes color.
  • You are using your rescue inhaler more often than normal.

What to do:

  • Use your rescue inhaler as directed.
  • Call your doctor or clinic within 24 hours for instructions.

Your provider may add a short course of steroid pills or antibiotics if needed.

Red Zone ‚Äì Emergency (Call 911 or go to Emergency Room)

  • Very hard time breathing, even at rest.
  • Chest pain, blue lips or fingertips.
  • Too short of breath to speak in full sentences.
  • Rescue inhaler is not helping or wears off very quickly.

This is an emergency. Call 911 or have someone take you to the nearest Emergency Room.

5. Other Medicines You May Receive

During or after your hospital stay, you may receive:

  • Steroid pills (for example, prednisone) to reduce swelling in the lungs.
  • Antibiotics if there is a lung infection (pneumonia or bronchitis).

Always finish these medicines as instructed unless your doctor tells you to stop.

6. Smoking & Lung Health

If you smoke, quitting is the most important thing you can do to slow down COPD.

  • Ask your care team about nicotine patches, gum, or other quit-smoking medicines.
  • Call the quitline (for example, 1-800-QUIT-NOW) for free support.
